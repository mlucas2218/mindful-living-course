Resources

Venturing beyond what's covered in class and exploring other sources is one of the best ways to learn about what's most interesting to YOU. There are an incredible number of books, documentaries, Internet articles, and organizations around the world with vast amounts of information and wisdom relating to various aspects of mindful living.

The resources listed here are just a few that I've found to be informative, accessible, and engaging, but they should be enough to get you started!

Books

Clean: The Revolutionary Program to Restore the Body's Natural Ability to Heal Itself

Alejandro Junger, 2011

A discussion of modern food, global toxicity, their effects on human health, and the body's natural detoxification system, followed by recipes, instructions, and advice for restoring your body to its natural state of optimal health through Dr. Junger's 3-week Clean detoxification program and beyond.

Cradle to Cradle: Remaking the Way We Make Things

William McDonough and Michael Braungart, 2002

McDonough and Braungart challenge the wasteful modern "cradle-to-grave" manufacturing model, as well as the "reduce, reuse, recycle" environmentalist mantra that aims to do "less bad" instead of being 100% good. They use the principle of "waste = food" to present a practical, realistic way to design products intelligently and achieve true eco-effectiveness.

Everything I Want to Do is Illegal: War Stories from the Local Food Front

Joel Salatin, 2007

A humorous, passionate discussion of past experiences at the Polyface family farm, the present situation of government regulations and cultural (mis)perceptions, and the future outlook on freedom for traditional food growing and purchasing choices. Salatin makes the average family farmer's experiences relatable to the average American consumer and sheds light on "the everyday conflict between the entrenched industrial food system and the local artisinal neighbor-friendly farmer-entrepreneur."

Nourishing Traditions: The Cookbook that Challenges Politically Correct Nutrition and the Diet Dictocrats

Sally Fallon, 1999

This well-researched, thought-provoking guide to traditional foods contains a startling message: Animal fats and cholesterol are not villains but vital factors in the diet, necessary for normal growth, proper function of the brain and nervous system, protection from disease and optimum energy levels. Sally Fallon dispels the myths of the current low-fat fad in this practical, entertaining guide to a can-do diet that is both nutritious and delicious. (description from Amazon.com)

The Omnivore's Dilemma: A Natural History of Four Meals

Michael Pollan, 2006

An insightful journey following each of the three main human food chains (industrial, organic, and hunter-gatherer) from beginning to end, i.e. plant to meal. Pollan demonstrates how in order to answer "What should I eat?", one must first explore: "What am I eating? And where in the world did it come from?"

The One-Straw Revolution: An Introduction to Natural Farming

Masanobu Fukuoka, 1975

The story of the development of Mr. Fukuoka's highly successful natural farming method in the mountains near Kyoto, Japan and its impact on the land, himself, and thousands of people he has taught. This book extends beyond practical farming knowhow to discuss diet, health, cultural values, and the philosophical limits of human knowledge in the grand scheme of the world.

Documentaries

Food, Inc. (2008): 1 hr 34 min

Drawing on the nonfiction books Fast Food Nation and The Omnivore's Dilemma, director Robert Kenner's provocative, Oscar-nominated documentary looks at the food industry's detrimental effects on human health and the environment. (description from Netflix.com)

 

Fresh (2009): 1 hr 17 min

This absorbing documentary surveys American farmers' and researchers' pioneering efforts to develop efficient systems for growing food. All of those profiled share a common goal of limiting pollution while creating healthier products. (description from Netflix.com)

 

Genetic Roulette (2012): 1 hr 25 min

Never-before-seen-evidence points to genetically engineered foods as a major contributor to rising disease rates in the US population, especially among children. Gastrointestinal disorders, allergies, inflammatory diseases, and infertility are just some of the problems implicated in humans, pets, livestock, and lab animals that eat genetically modified soybeans and corn. (description from the film website)

Hungry for Change (2012): 1 hr 29 min

This documentary exposes secrets the diet, weight loss and food industries don't want consumers to know about: deceptive strategies designed to keep you coming back for more. Find out what's keeping people from having the body and health they want. (description from Netflix.com)

 

King Corn (2007): 1 hr 30 min

In Aaron Woolf's thought-provoking documentary, friends Ian Cheney and Curt Ellis move back to America's Corn Belt to plant an acre of the nation's most-grown and most-subsidized grain and follow their crop into the U.S. food supply. (description from Netflix.com)

 

Tapped (2009): 1 hr 15 min

The high cost -- to both the environment and our health -- of bottled water is the subject of this documentary that enlists activists, environmentalists, community leaders and others to expose the dark side of the bottled water industry. Americans may rethink their obsession with bottled H20 when they learn of the unregulated industry's willingness to ignore environmental and health concerns, and the problems that arise as a result. (description from Netflix.com)

 

TED Talks

"Patriotism on a Plate"

Robyn O'Brien at TEDxMileHigh, 2011

Do you know what you are eating? In this extraordinary personal account, Robyn O'Brien [a Jones Business School alum!] tells the story of how she started paying attention to what's in food. The answer may surprise you and it will certainly inspire you to be more deliberate about your food choices. (description from TED.com)

"Teach Every Child About Food"

Jamie Oliver, 2010

Sharing powerful stories from his anti-obesity project in Huntington, W. Va., TED Prize winner Jamie Oliver makes the case for an all-out assault on our ignorance of food. (description from TED.com)

"Localisation is a Multiplier"

Ella Lawton at TEDxQueenstown, 2013

The Research Manager at Otago Polytechnic's Centre for Sustainable Practice in New Zealand, Ella Lawton discusses findings from the New Zealand Footprint Project and shares her vision of how each person can reduce their carbon footprint, help cultivate a sense of both individual and community well-being, and enrich one's overall quality of life by learning to eat and live locally.

Real Food Houston
